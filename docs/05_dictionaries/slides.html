Introduction to dictionaries

What is a dictionary?

  • a reference resource for language
  • typically organised alphabetically
  • provides definitions, etymologies, pronunciations, usage examples
  • can be specialised (e.g., technical dictionaries, slang dictionaries)
  • both research tool and primary source for linguistic research

Dictionary types

  • monolingual vs. bilingual or multilingual
  • synchronic (current language) vs. diachronic (historical development)
  • prescriptive (how language should be used) vs. descriptive (how language is used)
  • general purpose vs. specialised (technical, slang, etc.)
  • paper vs. electronic/online

Using dictionaries in linguistic research

Research applications

  • tracking lexical innovation and semantic change
  • studying etymology and historical development
  • analysing definitions and their evolution
  • investigating usage examples and contextual information
  • examining pronunciation patterns and variations
  • exploring semantic relations between words
  • researching register and dialect variations

Key features for linguistic research

  • historical coverage: first attestations and etymology
  • sense development: how meanings evolve over time
  • usage labels: indications of register, style, region
  • citations: authentic usage examples
  • pronunciation: phonetic transcriptions
  • grammatical information: part of speech, inflections

Prominent dictionary resources

The Oxford English Dictionary (OED)

  • historical dictionary of English
  • traces the history and development of English words
  • covers more than 1,000 years of language development
  • over 600,000 words defined
  • 3.5 million quotations illustrating word usage
  • available online (subscription required)

OED contains: - etymologies (word origins) - pronunciation guides - detailed definitions showing semantic development - dated quotations showing usage over time

Urban Dictionary

  • crowd-sourced online dictionary founded in 1999
  • focuses on slang and cultural phrases
  • anyone can submit and define terms
  • entries voted up or down by users
  • valuable resource for contemporary slang and neologisms
  • requires critical evaluation (no editorial control)

Examples and practice

Example dictionary entry analysis

For the word woke:

Traditional dictionary (OED): - traces etymology (from awake) - documents semantic shift from literal to political meaning - provides dated citations showing evolution of usage - includes usage labels (originally AAVE, now political)

Urban Dictionary: - contains numerous user-submitted definitions - reflects contemporary cultural attitudes - shows competing definitions and interpretations - illustrates ideological dimensions of definition

Using dictionaries for linguistic research projects

Research approaches

  • lexicography: creating specialised dictionaries
  • corpus creation: using dictionaries to build word lists
  • semantic change: tracking meaning evolution
  • sociolinguistics: studying identity through language
  • neologism studies: examining word formation processes
  • register analysis: investigating domain-specific language

Guidelines for dictionary use in research

  • use multiple dictionaries for comprehensive coverage
  • consider the purpose and audience of the dictionary
  • examine dated examples to track change over time
  • be aware of prescriptive biases in older dictionaries
  • analyse the metalanguage of definitions
  • treat crowd-sourced dictionaries with critical awareness
